# Part 2

Meursault is arrested and held in prison, where he gradually adjusts to confinement, losing his usual sense of time and gradually growing indifferent to his loss of freedom. An examining magistrate and, later, a prison chaplain repeatedly try to get him to express remorse or turn to God, but he refuses to perform feelings he doesn't have. At trial, the prosecution spends more time portraying him as cold and unnatural - citing his lack of tears at his mother's funeral - than examining the actual killing. He is convicted and sentenced to death by guillotine. In his final days, he rejects the chaplain's attempts at religious consolation, has an outburst of anger, and afterward settles into a strange calm - accepting the universe's indifference and finding peace in it as his execution nears.
