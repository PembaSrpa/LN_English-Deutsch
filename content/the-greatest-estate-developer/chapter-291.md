# Reckless Driving is Bad (2)

---

“How about putting up a circuit-shaped coliseum for racing?”

“Excuse me?” Head Chief Cherokan cocked his head to the side at the smiling Lloyd. It was a strange and new suggestion. “Coliseum? A circuit for racing? What does that mean?”

“Simply put,” Lloyd explained, “It’s a stadium solely for speed racing.”

“A stadium? It’s a stadium meant to hold a race?”

“Yes.” Lloyd nodded, thinking that things were going somewhere. The chief was intrigued and expressed his curiosity just enough. Lloyd realized that he should strike when the iron was still hot. And so, he rattled on, “It’s a space reserved for running, and everyone can watch safely as it happens. The participants will be able to gallop without any interruption.”

“Hmm.” Cherokan mused. “A stadium… A space for anyone to run without restraint. Are you saying that is the solution to my problem?”

“Yes, chief.”

“What makes you say that? What’s your argument?” The chief frowned.

Adding another layer of shamelessness to his face, Lloyd said, “Chief, you’ve told me how worried you are about the young centaurs running recklessly around and getting into accidents, big and small, while racing one another to win someone’s hand in marriage.”

“I did.”

“Exactly,” Lloyd said intently. “You’ve also said that you’re sorry to see these accidents killing or disabling those youths who would otherwise have had a bright future ahead of them. So imagine what it would be like if the young centaurs could freely gallop to their heart’s content in a stadium specially designed for running?”

“Hmm, you mean that there would be fewer accidents since they’d only gallop at full speed in a safe, designated area?”

“That’s right, chief.”

Despite Lloyd’s confidence, Cherokan looked hardly convinced. “But you seem to be forgetting something, my friend.”

“What is it, chief?” asked Lloyd.

“As you already know, we’ve lived our whole lives running wherever our hearts desired. Do you truly believe that we would be willing to only race inside a stadium? That won’t happen. Even with a stadium, we will still choose a random location to hold competitions, challenge each other, and race to determine who is faster. It’s just not in our blood to let someone run ahead of us.”

“Yes, I am aware of that, chief,” Lloyd replied. “That’s where the rules come in.”

“Rules?”

“Yes.”

“What rules?” The chief’s eyes sharpened.

Lloyd spoke as if the answer was obvious. “We will set a speed limit in certain locations.”

“Set a speed limit?”

“Yes. It’s simple. We’ll state that they can only trot in village areas, but the meadows are a free racing zone. That would be all the limits they need to comply with.”

“But that’s problematic,” Cherokan insisted.

“Why is that?”

“If meadows have no speed limits, then that renders the stadium unnecessary.”

“You’re right, but that can be solved with one extra regulation.”

“Which is?”

“To designate the coliseum as the only venue for holding marriage-related races.”

“So courtship competitions can only happen there…?”

“That is exactly it.” Lloyd could sense that the chief was starting to come around. It was now time for Lloyd to bring up the crux of the matter. “The most dangerous races are those for someone’s hand in marriage. Was that not the case with you, chief?”

“Hmm… Come to think of it, yes. I think so. It applied to my own proposal. I remember my competitiveness peaked at that age. It was so powerful that it was blinding. I successfully proposed after giving my all to win the race.”

“I figured. That’s also why it’s dangerous.” Lloyd pointed out.

That’s the way it is. It’s the same whether you’re a centaur or human.

A competition for someone’s hand in marriage. Such a race would naturally be fiercer than usual. That was easily understandable if you compared it to bar fights.

I’ve often seen similar instances in my country. Whenever guys start arguing with other guys at another table, all of them surprisingly play nice and try to move on because they don’t want to get into an unnecessary fight. Well, unless you’re a sadist who enjoys violence. However, there are exceptions.

It was when the guys were with girls.

The chances of a simple scuffle developing into something big were significantly higher.

In those situations, men suddenly loved to act tough and masculine before their love interests as they didn’t want to appear weak. They yielded less and regarded backing off as defeat, and would ultimately spoil the atmosphere of the bar with their heightened stubbornness and aggression.

It’s super childish and ridiculous. But it can’t be helped even if they’ll obviously regret their behavior in the future.

It occurred surprisingly frequently. At least based on Lloyd’s observation.

A real man should maintain reason and deal with the situation tactfully. But that’s easier said than done. The centaurs are quite similar. Even the novel mentions that these creatures become the most aggressive during marriage-related matters.

Lloyd recalled the story in the novel. It was a short explanation of the centaurs and how their innate love for speed and racing dramatically spiked when there was a potential marriage at stake. The only thing in their sight was victory and marrying their desired partner. It wasn’t uncommon for them to risk their lives.

If you think about it, it actually makes sense. I’d do the same. If I’ve got a competitor for the girl I want to marry, I’d obviously run like a madman. I wouldn’t care what’s ahead of me. I’d just run to cross the finish line first without a second thought.

Come hell or high water, the finish line would be the only thing in sight, and he would become blind to his surroundings, endangering himself and others around him.

“So,” Lloyd went on, “We’ll create a space exclusively for that reckless race for love. We’ll build a circuit-style coliseum.”

“Do you think that will reduce the number of accidents?”

“I believe it will. After all, the centaurs running at full speed will be separated from the other runners.”

It was the same case in a modern society like South Korea. There was a reason why highways existed apart from other roads.

There’s a notable example of this in Germany. The Autobahn. The road meant for drivers to speed on.

There were also similar places in South Korea. For instance, there was a circuit located in Inje County of the Gangwon Province.

The furrow between the head chief’s eyebrows deepened. Could it be that the chief somewhat understood Lloyd’s intention?

“I see what you mean. In short, you want to designate certain areas for different purposes. That’s why you’re suggesting this coliseum.”

“Yes, chief. Inside the village, everyone will have to trot slowly. In the meadow, they can canter or gallop. And only at the coliseum can they recklessly race over love.”

Cherokan nodded in understanding. “Good. I believe the number of tragic accidents will decrease if everyone follows these regulations.”

“It certainly looks like it.”

“Indeed. But… I am doubtful that my centaurs will follow your suggestions.” Chief Cherokan’s eyes faltered as they fixed on Lloyd. “As excellent as your ideas are, I can’t help but think they’re unrealistic. The question is whether these instinctive runners would willfully confine themselves and obey all the stifling rules. I personally do not think so.”

“You mean they’ll choose their right to freely gallop over their own safety.”

“Yes.” Chief Cherokan nodded. “The desire to gallop and race each other may be something humans will never be able to grasp fully. But that’s who we are. We’d rather die than be forbidden to run.”

“Hmm.” Lloyd stroked his chin.

“So, you can expect no young centaur to ever follow your rules if you designate a no-gallop zone and restrict areas for courtship races. They will ignore or protest with a scoff. That’s how intense their running instinct is.”

“Hmm…”

“Moreover,” Chief Cherokan went on, “This is about tradition. Starting from our earliest ancestors, we’ve lived freely around the land. That’s our traditional way of life. And your recommendations suggest changing that precedent.”

“Hmm…”

“And that is why, even if I like your suggestion, I’m not confident they will take up on it. Everyone will oppose it. There’s no reason for them to follow the rules.”

“Hmmmm…”

“My friend, why are you looking at me strangely?” asked Chief Cherokan.

“Oh, yes,” replied Lloyd. “It’s because I feel like I’m looking at a motorcycle gang that is desperately giving excuses to defend themselves.”

“A motorcycle gang?”

“Yes. Well. Something like that.” A chuckle escaped Lloyd. He couldn’t help it.

It’s like he’s telling me that they can’t help being carefree souls, and it’s not any of my business whether they live or die while running.

That was the impression Lloyd got after hearing Chief Cherokan’s explanation of the nature of centaurs.

Well. A speeding ticket being their glorious symbol already says a lot about them.

Of course, Lloyd couldn’t care less about whether these chronic speeders got killed or injured from recklessly racing. That would have been his normal reaction. But not now.

Chief said he’d tell me where Taupo was if I helped him develop a solution for safety. So I’ve got to create one for them.

Lloyd decided. He would help them improve safety. So he began to rack his brain, going over a list of possible things he could do and how they might be received. Once Lloyd finished doing the math in his head, he looked straight into the chief’s eyes. “How about this, chief? If you’re not confident you could persuade them, then I’ll do it for you.”

“You? You’ll persuade them?”

“Yes.”

“How?”

“You do not need to worry about that. I’ll just ask you one thing. If I get the centaurs to comply with the regulations and build the coliseum, can you promise me you’ll tell me where the Taupo is?”

That was the moment for him to push back. Lloyd’s voice swelled with confidence. Thankfully, it didn’t take a long time until the chief spoke.

“Fine… I promise, if you can persuade everyone to follow the planned regulations.”

“Very well,” Lloyd said. “Let’s take care of this then.”

Flap! Lloyd pulled out a piece of paper from his pocket once the chief nodded. Like a spider detecting food on its web or a python spotting its meal for the day, Lloyd held out a piece of paper without giving any time for Cherokan to react.

“This paper details the promises you’ve just made, chief. So why don’t we draw up two contracts? One for me and another for you.”

“Contracts…?”

“Yes.” Lloyd beamed and spoke nonchalantly. “It’s always better to handle these matters with as much clarity as possible. Oh, don’t get me wrong. I’m not doing this because I can’t trust you, chief. It’s the opposite. What if I go back on my words? These pieces of paper will help us establish healthy and mutual trust.”

“…”

Gulp. Chief Cherokan wondered for a second whether he was in the presence of a scammer. He had spent his whole life living a simple life in the meadows and couldn’t help having such a suspicion. Despite his anxiety, they soon signed and finalized the contract at the speed of light.

“Okay, please write your name here. The date. Your signature. All right. Just stamp right over here.”

“…”

“Oh my, look at that. That was a perfectly placed stamp,” praised Lloyd. “As I thought, you can really assess someone’s disposition by seeing how they sign a contract. I can tell you’re a considerate, meticulous, and cheerful centaur from this. And you’re obviously popular among centaurs, both male and female, because of your thoughtfulness.”

“I-Is that so?” muttered Chief Cherokan.

“Yes. I’m not trying to blow my own trumpet, but I’m a very good judge of character.”

“Ehem! Ehem! Come to think of it, you’re right. Hahaha.”

“Right? I’ve read you correctly, chief.”

“Hahaha! Thank you!”

“Well then, I shall be going now. I’ll come back once I persuade your tribe members.”

Lloyd smiled as he rose up. The contract was signed. It was over. Lloyd bolted out of the place before the nice mood he worked so hard to create turned sour. When he walked out of the stable, he was greeted by Javier who had been waiting nearby.

“Are you done talking to him?” asked Javier.

“Yeah. You heard most of the conversation, right?

"Yes.” Javier nodded as he walked beside Lloyd. “But that is why I am more confused. I cannot understand why you would build a coliseum for them and make the tribe follow the safety regulations.”

“Why? Is it strange?”

“Yes.”

“In what way?”

“As far as I am concerned, I do not think anyone will obey it.”

“Yes, you’re probably right.” Lloyd didn’t hesitate to confirm Javier’s opinion.

Lloyd felt the stinging gaze from Javier. Javier finally asked, “I am probably right? You speak as though you already have an idea what will happen.”

“Yeah,” acknowledged Lloyd. “I do.”

He really did. Lloyd was already aware of how the centaurs would react even before he suggested the coliseum project and safety regulations to the chief.

“Then I suppose you have a secret plan in mind?” asked Javier.

“Uh-huh.”

“May I ask what that is?”

“Yeah. Sure. I’m going to run a safe racing campaign and provoke the entire tribe.”

“Provoke the entire tribe, Young Master?” Javier tilted his head in confusion.

“It means I plan to piss everyone off,” Lloyd said cheerfully.
