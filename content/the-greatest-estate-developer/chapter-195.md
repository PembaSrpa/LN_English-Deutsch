# Drillers (2)

---

“Once upon a time, a long, long time ago, the water quality of the treated water was comparatively good, and the slow filtration method was adopted when the E. coli group was less than 1,000 (100mL, MPN), biological oxygen demand (BOD) was less than 2 mg/L, and the maximum turbidity was less than 10 degrees.”

“Hold on…”

“Raw water treatment method aims at removing small amounts of turbidity and traces of organic matter from raw groundwater, eutrophication-free dam water, lake water, and pollution-free river water. The slow filtration method slowly filters raw water through a relatively thin layer of sand…”

“St…op…”

“This action also removes trace amounts of ammonia nitrogen, manganese, bacteria, and odor substances from the turbidity. Sedimentation paper is unnecessary at a maximum turbidity of 50°F or less per year, normal sedimentation paper is installed at a maximum turbidity of 50°F to 86°F per year, and a sedimentation paper capable of drug treatment is installed at a maximum turbidity of 50°F or higher per year.”

Zzz... Sheherazade’s head roughly jerked back, and her snores rang aloud the room. A satisfied smile appeared on Lloyd’s mouth.

Perfect.

Judging by how she fell asleep, it was obvious that the afternoon had been exhausting for her. Probably due to Javier’s strenuous sword lessons. Lloyd turned and looked at Javier, who was standing beside him, and then, he motioned a message with his hand.

“It’s over. Open your ears.”

Javier reacted to the hand motion by opening his ears which he had purposefully blocked off.

“Is it over?” asked Javier.

“Yeah. Why, did you want to listen to the lullaby as well?”

“No, not at all.” Javier shook his head with a serious look on his face.

The corners of Lloyd’s lips pulled further up mischievously.

“Not at all? Really? Truly?”

“Yes, without a doubt.”

“Be honest with me. I know you want to listen to my lullaby. Right?” insisted Lloyd.

“Not at all.”

“Just imagine it. You fall into a nice, peaceful sleep. And as the soft lullaby fills your ears, you slowly travel to your dreamland where pain or stress doesn’t exist. Completely relaxed. You’re so relaxed that even the dead skin on your feet let loose. It’s soothing. Warm. Comfortable. Fluffy. Imagine it in your head. Wow. Doesn’t it sound awesome?”

“…”

“Hey, did you know your eyes slightly closed just now?”

“…”

“My, my. No need to force them open now.”

“…”

“And now, you’re glowering at me.”

“Haah… Come on.”

Javier finally let out a sigh. He hated Lloyd. Hated him so much. If only he could, he wanted to flick Lloyd on the forehead. He would have done so if the latter wasn’t his young master. But now, he didn’t have any time to dwell on it. The time to work had arrived.

“So, do I have to repeat what I did yesterday?” asked Javier.

“Uh-huh.”

Javier immediately put aside his boiling rage toward Lloyd and calmly asked about his tasks for the day in a professional manner. Lloyd nodded.

“You’ll see the markings I made in the afternoon.”

“Should the size and slope of the tunnels remain the same?” asked Javier again.

“Yeah. It’s all marked the same as yesterday. So, you can just follow them as they are.”

“Noted.”

Javier got up from his seat and took his sword. Just like the past few days, he wore a robust leather vest on a comfortable shirt and put on a mask that would protect his nose and mouth from the thick layers of dust.

“I suggest you take a break for a bit,” suggested Javier to Lloyd.

“Yeah. You should be careful too.”

“Yes.”

Master Lloyd will come out after an hour or two of sleep like usual. So, my job is to dig the designated areas until he comes back and drill the grounds exactly the way they are designed. Javier walked out of the room thinking so, heading to the field where excavation was taking place to build the Qanat. As he traveled there, the events that had taken place came to his mind one by one.

Has it been two months already?

It had been two months since he arrived in this city. In the first month, he had to spend his time shut inside the room. His daily routine involved dealing with Sheherazade while Lloyd was out surveying and designing. Thankfully, she didn’t bore him. Their fights offered him an adequate warm-up exercise and a source of stimulation to improve his swordsmanship. Meanwhile, Lloyd finished surveying the underground tunnels and designing the Qanat. He had shared with Javier that he managed to design a short underground tunnel that would connect the intake station, which would be built at the foot of the western mountain range, to this city. The distance between them was about 23 miles.

The last time I had a proper night of sleep was a month ago.

In the second month of his stay, sleep came rare. The reason was simple. In the afternoon, he was forced to confine Sheherazade as he sword-fought her. At night, he headed to the construction field and dug tunnels. The toil placed a heavy burden on him, leading him to feel extreme fatigue. He even sometimes felt dizzy, and it was the case even when he owned the manaheart of massive density that surpassed any ordinary sword masters and supplemented his lacking stamina using the Asrahan Core Technique. But he barely gave a hang about exhaustion. No, he was actually enjoying this situation. Even when there was a way of knocking her cold fast and taking some breaks, he forced himself to melee for several hours and strained himself on purpose.

In the end, just overcoming extreme fatigue will also serve as a form of training for me.

He wouldn’t relax just because he was a sword master. He would push his Asrahan Core Technique to higher levels. For that, he had to resort to extraordinary methods. A half-hearted effort wouldn’t suffice. Javier was painfully aware of that fact. He once pushed the limits of being human to become a sword master, and now that he was aiming higher, he had to completely break free from the shackles of man. He had to overcome all the obstacles in his way. He had to defeat them. Reach where no one had ever reached. He was challenging himself to a level no one had ever treaded or reached before. This was the transcendental level of power he was aiming for. So, he accepted that his current suffering and struggle were only natural. He resolved to push forward today like any other day and arrived at the construction site.

“I heard you can start the job from back there. Please follow me.”

Javier was guided by a man in the rebel group who was guarding the site. They walked the long path of the tunnel, and Javier spotted the traces of Lloyd’s work in the afternoon.

Master Lloyd is closing all the paths that won’t be used as the waterway.

Bricks and cement were noticeable throughout, blocking the path that was originally opened up. Javier postulated that it was to control the water inside the Qanat and prevent it from flowing elsewhere.

Master Lloyd must have put his nose to the grindstone all day.

To an untrained eye, those bricks and cement meant nothing. Just an object blocking the tunnel. But Javier was different. He had come to know how challenging waterproofing can get, thanks to his experiences in construction. The cement and brick walls looked simple to put up, but they were actually the results of hard work. They were the byproducts of countless geometrical calculations, careful deliberation, and seasoned experience. But it wasn’t only the design that was tricky. The construction part was the same. After all, a good design didn’t vouch for good results. That was why construction demanded much more care, effort, and deliberation than designing. First, the volcanic ash Bangul excreted must be gathered. Second, the cement must be mixed lest it cracks. Third, the size and angle of the bricks must be exact, not a single location or angle, or thickness wrong. No small gap or hole where water could leak into. Every process of stacking, plastering, and inspecting required thorough care.

I wonder where Master Lloyd learned all of this.

Master Lloyd was nothing but a party animal who liked to waste his years indulging in pleasure. Where did he learn these things without his and the Frontera family’s knowledge?

Once, he gave me an excuse that he learned everything in a mysterious school in his dream.

A memory popped into Javier’s head just then. It was probably around when Viscount Lacona messed up the river and Lloyd constructed the water system to deal with the contaminated river. Javier had asked Lloyd where he had learned of all these. Lloyd answered in a joking manner that he dreamed about it. In a dream, he became a student at a mysterious university and learned all of these about construction. That was how he could do these things.

Could it be that he wasn’t joking?

During that time, Javier thought Lloyd was throwing a joke to fool him. But now, such a ridiculous answer sounded plausible.

“This is it.”

The voice of the rebel broke Javier’s trance, and he looked up. He was already at the end of the tunnel and saw various half-transparent signs marked on the wall. The dots, lines, lines, and sides glowed in faint blue. It was the guideline Lloyd had left for Javier. And on the side, he spotted a note. It was a memo instructing about the job.

[I’m going to get some sleep while you dig this. Good luck.]

“…”

Should I go back and punch his face? Javier tightened his grip on the sword.

The Qanat construction continued. The process was similar every day. When the sun was down, Javier headed to the construction site and dug following Lloyd’s construction guidelines. A mana blast, precise and controlled, was blasted each time his sword shone. A 65-foot hole was dug in a flash. Its width was about 23 inches, and 3 feet in height. It was a narrow path suitable for only one person to pass through. It looked too narrow at first glance, but the fact was that the tunnel was dug to maximize the construction’s efficiency and safety.

Of course. There’s a higher chance of a collapse if the tunnel is dug wider than this.

The pressure inside the tunnel increased as it got wider. As a result, the chances of a collapse grew higher as the weight became too heavy to bear. That and Lloyd’s goal to end this project as fast as possible were the reasons behind appropriately sizing the tunnels that would later become waterways. The tunnel sizes needed to meet only two criteria to be appropriate. The maximum size had to be independently strong enough to bear the load weight without any help from support structures, such as steel ribs, shotcretes, and rock bolts. At the same time, it had to contain enough space for a person to pass through for maintenance after the construction was complete. The result was a tunnel that was 23 inches in width and 3 feet in height. Of course, Lloyd had run a host of simulations before making this. He calculated the chances of each section collapsing after water flowed inside for several decades and centuries. He ran the tests countless times on the features of the soil and rocks existing in each section. Ultimately, it gave Javier even more work to do.

“Whew!”

The tunnel that was planned to be used as a waterway was too narrow. Javier had to squat down to move as it was not even 3 feet tall. And its 23 inches of width made it harder for him to turn around or use his sword. However, Javier, the Knight of Blood and Iron, didn’t even grumble. He regarded this averse condition as another training exercise that would help him overcome his limits as a sword master.

Boom! Javier’s mana blast extended the waterways with each burst. The extended channels connected with the main tunnel to create the shortest path possible. The connected waterways resembled underground capillaries. When Javier was finished digging through the night, Lloyd took up the baton and continued in the morning.

“Lalala! Lalalaa! Bangul!” exclaimed Lloyd.

“Bangul!”

Boom! Bangul’s volcanic ash was gathered and mixed into cement. And Lloyd closed several areas of the tunnel with cement and bricks to ensure the water didn’t take the wrong path.

If I don’t block them, it’s going to be a mess. If the water leaks somewhere odd and stagnates in a corner, it would become a breeding ground for germs.

Then the entire water of Qanat would be contaminated. In other words, the water that took so much pain to draw would become unusable. Thus, Lloyd tightly blocked every nook and corner of the underground tunnel that wouldn’t be used as a waterway. Just like that, the Knight of Blood and Iron blasted the tunnel while the Young Master sealed it. This process continued every day and night. Ten days. A fortnight. A month. Two months. The waterway that was first constructed near Kandara finally started to reach the foot of the mountain range in the west.

“Today, we are finally putting an end to this long construction project.”

On the day of the completion, Lloyd gathered Javier and the rebels in one place. He surveyed everyone at the upper ground of the mother well.

“It was not easy, but you all did a good job,” announced Lloyd in a serious voice.

A look of mixed emotions appeared on the faces of the rebels, including Termes. They had all worked together day and night covered in a thick layer of dust.

Of course. Without their help, the construction would have taken much longer.

Lloyd was grateful for the rebels. He wasn’t alone when he blocked the useless parts of the tunnels. These men had helped move all the cement and bricks, and it was they who transferred the excavated soil outside the tunnel. Aside from that, they drilled countless vertical holes that were 65 feet deep and 328 feet apart from each other, which would be used as a passage to go down to the waterways for future maintenance and repair.

They gladly took those jobs.

Construction wasn’t a one-man job. It required a host of workers teaming up and working together. Sometimes, in the process, quarrels and conflicts arose, and they fought over negligible profits and losses. But eventually, they had to work as one to complete the construction. That was the nature of construction work. That was what construction was.

“We all gladly covered ourselves in the dirt for our work until now. And so, I think we should enjoy the final moment of this construction together.”

“Do you mean that we shovel all together at the end of the waterway?” Termes asked after Lloyd finished his speech.

“No, that’s not the case. It’s going to be a disaster if we do that,” said Lloyd as he faintly smiled.

“Hmm? Disaster? What do you mean-”

“We’ll die.”

Termes became speechless. The corners of Lloyd’s lips pulled further up.

“I don’t know if you know,” explained Lloyd, “but only a bedrock that’s about 10 feet wide stands in between the end of our waterway and this mother well. But you see, that mother well goes deeper than 98 feet underground. Accordingly, it possesses a large amount of water and pressure.”

“Then if we burst the bedrock and connect the waterway-”

“Yes, you’re right. The water stagnating in the mother well will burst, sweeping the inside of the waterway with tremendous water pressure. You can imagine what would happen to the last person who bore the tunnel.”

Lloyd paused and motioned, slitting his throat with his hand.

“That’ll be the end of him.”

Lloyd was speaking the truth. The water pressure from connecting the waterway and the mother well was intolerable for humans. It would lead to immediate death, nothing more or less. So, the ancient laborers who actually created the Qanat were…

“Do you have any other method in mind?” asked Termes.

“Yes.”

Lloyd nodded. Of course, he had something in mind.

“I just need one volunteer to sacrifice.”

That was what the ancient workers did in the Middle East. The oldest laborer met his final moment when the Qanat was constructed. He pickaxed with dignity and composure, connecting the waterway and mother well. Then he died, sacrificing himself to give clean water to future generations and his descendants.

“However, well, we don’t need anyone to die.”

That was also obviously the case. This was not the ancient Middle East. This world was different. There wasn’t any need to sacrifice a healthy individual. This would be especially true if there was an entity that could do the job for them without getting harmed.

“Right?”

Lloyd grinned as he looked at the back of the rebel guys, who all turned back following his gaze. And beyond the mound of rocks in the mountain foot appeared a skull of a skeleton soldier whose neck was stuck out forward. And it shook its bony hand at everyone, making a finger heart sign like it had learned from Lloyd the other time.

Crack! It was Turtle Neck, the leader of the Skeleton Corps, that traveled from Frontera fiefdom and crossed the mountains and deserts to meet Lloyd.
